x Squared is Congruent to 1 iff x is Congruent to Plus or Minus One Mod p
\[ x ^ 2 \equiv 1 \pmod{ p } \iff x \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{ p } \]

\( \implies \) Suppose that \( x ^ 2 \equiv 1 \pmod{ p } \) therefore \( p \mid x ^ 2 - 1 = \left( x + 1 \right) \left( x - 1 \right) \) therefore \( p \mid \left( x + 1 \right) \) or \( p \mid \left( x - 1 \right) \) so that \( x \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{ p } \)

\( \impliedby \) Suppose that \( x \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{ p } \) therefore \( x ^ 2 \equiv 1 \pmod{ p } \) as needed.

Note that since \( p \nmid a \) we have that \[ a ^ { p - 1 } \equiv 1 \pmod{ p } \] since \( p - 1 \) is even, then \( 2 \) divides it so that \( \frac{ p - 1}{2 } \in \mathbb{Z } \), therefore we have \[ \left( a ^ { \frac{p - 1}{2} } \right) ^ 2 \equiv 1 \] therefore \[ a ^ { \frac{p - 1}{2} } \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{ p } \]
Number of Solutions to x Squared Congruent to Minus One Mod p
The equation \[ x ^ 2 \equiv \left( -1 \right) \pmod{ p } \] has two solutions explicitly given by \( \pm \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right)! \) (which are incongruent mod \( p \) ) iff \( p = 4k + 1 \)

\( \implies \) Suppose that \( x ^ 2 \equiv -1 \pmod{ p } \) has two solutions as stated in the question, suppose for the sake of contradiction that \( p = 4k + 3 \), then we have \[ \left[ \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \right] ^ 2 \equiv \left( -1 \right) ^ { \frac{4k + 3 + 1}{2} } \equiv 1 \pmod{ p } \] which is a contradiction because if \( p = 4k + 3 \) then \( p \ge 3 \) therefore \( -1 \not \equiv 1 \pmod{ p } \) but that is implied as through the assumption and the fact we just noticed above.

\( \impliedby \) Suppose that \( p = 4k + 1 \) and recall that this means that \[ \left[ \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \right] ^ 2 \equiv \left( -1 \right) ^ { \frac{4k + 1 + 1}{2} } \equiv -1 \pmod{ p } \] We'll prove that these two solutions are incongruent, first note that since \( p \ge 2 \) then we know that \( k \ge 1 \) therefore we know that \( p \ge 5 \) \[ 1 \le \frac{p - 1}{2} \lt p \] therefore \( p \nmid \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \), thus \( \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \) has an inverse, so then if they happened to be congruent we have \[ \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \equiv - \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \pmod{ p } \] which implies that \[ 1 \equiv -1 \pmod{ p } \] which is a contradiction since \( p \ge 5 \) therefore we must have that \[ \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \not \equiv - \left( \frac{p - 1}{2} \right) ! \pmod{ p } \]

a Squared b Squared
Suppose that \( p \in \mathbb{ P } _ 3 \) and that \( p \mid a ^ 2 + b ^ 2 \) such that \( a, b \in \mathbb{ Z } \) are coprime, prove that \( p \) is of the form \( 4k + 1 \)

If it so happens that \( a \) had an inverse mod \( p \) (denoted by \( c \)) since we know that \( a ^ 2 \equiv - b ^ 2 \pmod{ p } \) then we would be able to say that \[ -1 \equiv \left( b c \right) ^ 2 \pmod{ p } \] and therefore \( p \) is of the form \( 4k + 1 \).

Therefore one just has to verify that \( a \) is invertible mod \( p \), and actually if it was that \( b \) was invertible then we could have symmetrically done the same. Recall that a number is invertible mod \( p \) if \( p \nmid a \), so suppose for the sake of contradiction that \( p \mid a \) therefore \( p \mid a ^ 2 \) and since \( p \mid a ^ 2 + b ^ 2 \) then \( p \mid b ^ 2 \) but then \( p \mid b \) and so \( \gcd \left( a, b \right) \ge p \) which is a contradiction, therefore \( p \nmid a \), as needed.
