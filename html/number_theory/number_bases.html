base represenation
Suppose that `m, k in NN_1` and `(a_(k - 1), a_(k - 2), ..., a_(0)) in {0, 1, 2, ..., m}^k`. Then we define `n := sum_(i = 0)^(k - 1) a_i m^i` and we say that `a_(k - 1)a_(k - 2) ... a_(1)` is the representation of `n` in the base `m`
decimal representation
The decimal representation of a number `n` is the represetnation of `n` in the base `10`
The decimal representation of a number is the usual way you've most likely dealt with numbers so far, for example when you write `123` you are referring to the number `1 * 10^2 + 2* 10^1 + 3 * 10^0`
binary representation
The binary representation of a number `n` is the represetnation of `n` in the base `2`
division by 9 rule
Suppose that `a_(k -1) a_(k -2) ... a_1` is the decimal representation of `n`, then `9 | n` if and only if `9 | sum_(i = 0)^(k - 1) a_i`
