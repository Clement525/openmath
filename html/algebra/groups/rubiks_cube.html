Rubik's Cube Move Inverter

Cubie
A cubie is an atomic unit on a Rubik's Cube.
Algorithm
An algorithm is a finite sequence of moves
Rubiks Cube Base Moves
The moves of a rubiks cube \[ M = \{f, b, r, l, u, d, e\} \]
Move Operator
We define the move operator as follows (where the ) \[ \forall m \in M, m ^ 4 = e \] Also some of the elements in \(M \) commute \[ f \star b = b \star f \] \[ l \star r = r \star l \] \[ u \star d = d \star u \] aditionally the element \( e \) acts like an identity: \[ \forall m \in M, m \star e = e \star m = m \]
States of the Cube
We define the states of the cube as a set \( G \) \[ G = \left\{\prod _ {i = 1} ^n m_i : m_i \in M \text{ and } n \in \mathbb{N}_1 \right\} \]

Note that any state of the cube can be thought of as an ordered sequences of "moves" by reading from left to right which are being applied to \( e \)

The Rubiks Cube is a Group
The set \(M \) along with the move operator form a group.

  • Identity:
    Let \(g \in G\). By definition of \(G\), we know there exists \(m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_n \in M\) such that \(g = m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n\) for some \(n \in \mathbb{N}_1\). By definition of \(e\), \(m \star e = e \star m = m\) for all \(m \in M\). So by associativity,
    \[ \begin{aligned} e \star g &= e \star (m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n) \\ &= (e \star m_1) \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n \\ &= (m_1 \star e) \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n \\ &= m_1 \star (e \star m_2) \star \ldots \star m_n \\ &= m_1 \star (m_2 \star e) \star \ldots \star m_n \\ &= m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots (e \star m_n) \\ &= m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots (m_n \star e) \\ &= (m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n) \star e \\ &= g \star e \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \(e\) is the identity element in \(G\).
  • Inversion:
    Let \(m \in M \subset G\). Then, \(m \star m^3 = m^4 = e\) and \(m^3 \star m = m^4 = e\). So, \(m^{-1} = m^3\).

    Let \(g \in G\). By definition of \(G\), we know there exists \(m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_n \in M\) such that \(g = m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n\) for some \(n \in \mathbb{N}_1\). Now, consider \(g^\prime = m_n^3 \star \ldots \star m_2^3 \star m_1^3\). By associativity, we have the following. \[ \begin{aligned} g \star g^\prime &= (m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star m_n) \star (m_n^3 \star \ldots \star m_2^3 \star m_1^3) \\ &= m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star (m_n \star m_n^3) \star \ldots \star m_2^3 \star m_1^3 \\ &= m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star e \star m_{n-1}^3 \star \ldots \star m_2^3 \star m_1^3 \\ &= m_1 \star m_2 \star \ldots \star \ldots \star m_2^3 \star m_1^3 \\ \end{aligned} \]
